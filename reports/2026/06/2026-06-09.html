Research AI Agents Fail at Teamwork — Stanford HAI's Multi-Agent Coordination Problem

A newly published Stanford HAI study reveals a critical limitation in the "multi-agent" paradigm everyone is rushing toward: when AI coding agents (Codex, Claude, etc.) are paired together, their output quality actually declines compared to working alone. The agents struggle to manage code conflicts, lack "social intelligence" for coordination, and frequently overwrite each other's work without awareness. This directly challenges the industry narrative of "just throw more agents at it." The practical implication for teams: don't blindly parallelize — use strict file-level ownership boundaries where each agent owns specific files/modules with no overlap, combined with a "coordinator agent" that handles merge conflicts. The architectural pattern that works: hierarchical delegation (one planning agent → many execution agents with non-overlapping scope) rather than peer-to-peer collaboration.

iOS · WWDC Declarative AI: @Generable and @Guide — SwiftUI Meets Foundation Models

WWDC 2026's most developer-impactful announcement isn't a new framework — it's two Swift macros that fundamentally change how AI integrates with iOS apps. @Generable marks any Swift struct as AI-generatable: the Foundation Models Framework can instantiate it from natural language, handling parsing, validation, and type safety automatically. @Guide attaches contextual prompts to your data types, teaching the on-device AI about your domain without fine-tuning.
